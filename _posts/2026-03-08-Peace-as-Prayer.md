---

**Lord, teach me the difference between restlessness and obedience.**

I've been reading Father Walter Ciszek's *He Leadeth Me*, and one passage struck me like a bell. He writes of his doubts—not the sanitized, faith-hero kind, but real doubt. Should he go to Russia? The question tormented him. But then he describes the fruit of the spirit test: *peace*. Not certainty. Not the absence of fear. But peace—a deep knowing that rests underneath the trembling.

I think you've been teaching me that same test, Lord. Not through dramatic calls, but through the ordinary graces of my life.

Last week, my family was playing a game. The room filled with laughter—the kind that rises unbidden from joy, not performance. My children were present. My spouse was present. I was present. And in that moment, I felt you so clearly that it took my breath away. Not as a question I had to answer, but as a *presence* I could simply recognize.

This is what you've been trying to show me: **that you speak not in the absence of life, but within its fabric.**

I'm learning to notice when you show up. You're there in the laughter. You're there when I choose an activity not because It's right, but because in it I find *peace*. The peace that says: *This is where I become more myself, more present, more capable of loving.* 

But there are other voices now, Lord, and they're growing louder.

My in-laws are evangelizing their faith with vigor, conviction, certainty. They speak from love—I know this. And I'm trying to hold space for their calling while also protecting something tender in my own family. The way of the Church. The way of sacrament and mystery. The way that says we don't have all the answers, and that's okay.

Father Ciszek knew this tension. He knew the question of obedience when the call comes from outside—when it might ask us to go somewhere, believe something, become someone in ways we didn't choose. His answer wasn't rebellion. It wasn't blind compliance either.

**It was discernment.**

Lord, I'm asking you now: Help me recognize your voice when the air is full of other voices. Help me know the difference between:
- The peace that comes from belonging to you
- The anxiety that comes from trying to please everyone
- The restlessness that comes from losing my center in you

Help my family find peace in our own path, even as we honor the faith journeys of those we love.

Here's what I'm noticing, and it humbles me: I'm finally doing things *for their own sake*—not to earn your favor, not to prove my devotion, not to fill the void. I'm doing them because in the doing, I feel peace. I think this is what maturity in faith looks like. Not more striving. Not more perfectionism. But a kind of surrender where the doing and the being become one.

When I'm laughing with my family, I'm not checking a box called "Love my Family." I'm *loving*. And you're there in it, not as a demand I'm meeting, but as the deepest ground of the joy itself. 

But Lord, there's one thing I can't seem to release, and I need to say it plainly in this prayer: **The distance from my parents is the hardest part.**

The anger is still there. I carry it like a stone in my chest. I understand now that some of it isn't really about what they did or didn't do—it's about the impossibility of holding them, of fixing it, of going back and making it different. It's the anger of a child who couldn't save her parents, and now must live with that.

I'm trying to learn what you're asking: *patience*. *Trust*. The acceptance that "all things have a season."

But patience feels like forgetting. Trust feels like not caring anymore. And seasons—the word aches in my throat because it means accepting that some things don't return.

Help me, Lord. Help me understand that *season* doesn't mean *loss*. That *distance* can hold love. That my anger, when brought to you, can become something else.

And here's the whisper underneath it all, the one I'm almost ashamed to name: **I'm afraid that if I re-engage with life—with my interests, my activities, my own becoming—I'll lose sight of you again.**

I've tasted what it's like to wander. To go through the motions. To be present in body but absent in spirit. Some part of me thinks that if I step away from my vigilance, my prayer, my constant turning toward you, I'll slip backwards. That I'll become functional but hollow. 

But Father Ciszek teaches me differently. He shows me a man who found you in a gulag. A man who found you in obedience that wasn't his choice. A man who learned that *you are not small enough to lose if I stop gripping.*

So I'm trying to believe: The peace I feel in laughter, in play, in genuine engagement with my life—*that's not a distraction from you. That's how you're present now.*

Lord, help me notice your peace without grasping it. Help me recognize your voice in the quiet moments and in the noisy ones, in the laughter and in the silence. Help me trust that you're not hiding, even when I'm busy, even when I'm fully alive, even when I'm not consciously seeking.

Help my family navigate the tensions that come when different people are listening for different voices—not with fear, but with the confidence that you're not small, and you don't compete. Help me hold my parents in my heart without needing to hold them in my hands. Teach me patience that isn't resignation but acceptance. Teach me that *season* is not another word for *goodbye*.

And Lord, help me understand that the anger I carry toward those I couldn't save is itself a kind of love. Help me transform it—not by denying it, but by bringing it to you, the only One who can hold all the unbearable things.

In this season, teach me to recognize your signature: **peace that steadies, presence that doesn't demand, and a voice that sounds like home because, in the end, you are.**

When you don't know the way forward, Father Ciszek teaches us, look for the fruit: *love, joy, peace, patience, kindness, goodness, faithfulness, gentleness, self-control.* Not certainty. Not a clear map. But the fruit. I'm learning to taste it. To notice when it's present and when it's absent. To trust it as your signature.

This past week, something became crystal clear. I noticed a pattern—and patterns, I'm learning, are how you whisper to us if we're paying attention.

**When I'm in the flow of an activity, being interrupted is like being grabbed while swimming.** It's startling. I'm left gasping for breath. The disruption doesn't just pause what I'm doing; it shatters something tender. It takes me out of the presence I've fought to build.

This matters. It's not just about being focused—it's about the *grace* of being fully where I am. When I'm interrupted, I lose not just the activity but the peace itself.

So this week, I'm experimenting with something small but significant: **incorporating faith and family time directly into the flow, rather than compartmentalizing it.** Not praying *then* living, but letting the two become one rhythm. What if the sacred isn't something I have to protect from interruption, but something that *lives in* the ordinary moments?

And yesterday, **I stayed connected with our family and guests by doing the dishes after dinner.**

Not escaping to do them. Not checking out. But staying in the kitchen, in the conversation, in the presence. My hands were busy, but I wasn't running. I was *there*. And in that small, humble act—the warmth of the water, the rhythm of washing, the nearness of people I love—I felt you so clearly.

**Lord, thank you for teaching me to recognize your voice.** Not in the thunder, but in the peace. Not in the certainty, but in the trust. Not in my striving, but in my becoming.

Help me protect the sacred by living it, not by hiding it. Help me notice your signature in flow, in presence, in the willingness to stay.

Lead me still.  Your son.
**Amen.**
