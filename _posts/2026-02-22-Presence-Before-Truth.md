**Dear Lord,**

I made it to confession this weekend.

That sentence feels small, but it wasn’t easy. I walked in carrying more than I wanted to admit—impatience, control, subtle resentment, the quiet desire to be right more than to be holy.

Afterward, I picked up a small cross made of knotted cord. Each knot represents our sins. They tangle. They tighten. They compound.

And Christ enters the tangle.

He doesn’t stand outside it waiting for us to clean it up. He steps into the mess and carries the cross with us.

Holding that little cross, I realized some of my knots aren’t dramatic failures. They’re quieter. The need to fix. The urgency to correct. The impulse to manage outcomes so I don’t have to feel helpless.

I’m learning something uncomfortable: presence comes before truth.

Truth matters. It matters eternally. But truth forced into a heart that doesn’t feel safe only hardens it. Love prepares the ground. Presence softens the soil. Only then can truth take root.

I see how often I’ve tried to strategize my way to healing—at home, in extended family, even in faith. If I just explain it better. If I just say it clearly enough. If I just hold the line harder.

But You are not in a hurry.

And when I move faster than love, I lose You.

During service, I found myself silently wishing peace over Missy. Over the kids. Over my parents. Over her parents. It’s lonely walking into church alone some Sundays. I feel that ache. I feel the temptation to turn that loneliness into quiet judgment or self-pity.

But marriage, I’m learning, isn’t something I extract from. It’s something I give into.

Faith, too, isn’t something I wield. It’s something I surrender into.

I don’t want my longing for appearances—a family sitting neatly together in a pew—to distract me from the deeper work of loving the family I actually have. If I focus on optics instead of intimacy, I will miss the sacred ground right in front of me.

Lord, continue to steady me as I hold difficult boundaries with my parents. They don’t understand why distance has been necessary. They expect obligation in spite of my strain. They want loyalty even if its only a performance.

I cannot make them see what I see.

I can only choose to become someone more present, not more defensive.

The demand to perform—to be the good son, the agreeable one, the strong one—pulls me away from tenderness. It makes me sharper than I want to be. Less patient. Less kind.

So I am learning to disappoint people without abandoning love.

That is a new muscle.

This Sunday, I’m dedicating the day to You and to the stewardship of my family. Not to proving anything. Not to solving everything. Just to staying.

Thinking of You.
Thinking of them.
Serving quietly.

Walking softly. Waiting for invitation instead of forcing entry.

I used to believe faith meant having answers.

Now I think it means staying in the tension long enough for grace to work.

If someone reading this feels tired—tired of trying to fix everything, tired of carrying everyone’s expectations, tired of managing outcomes—you don’t need a new strategy. You need a posture.

Kneel.

Not because you’ve failed beyond repair, but because you are tangled beyond your own strength.

Let Christ enter the knots.

Let Him carry what you cannot untie.

You do not have to be perfect to be faithful.

You only have to stay.

I am far from perfect.
But I am forgiven.

And today, that is enough.

**I love You, Lord.**
Avi
