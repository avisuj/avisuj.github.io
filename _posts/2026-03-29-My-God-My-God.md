My couples therapist asked me this week whether I had processed the worst thing that happened to my daughter.

I told her I hadn't. Not really. I've been processing everything around it — the foundation of my marriage, my relationship with my children, my relationship with my parents. But not that. I've been building a perimeter around the center and calling it healing.

Palm Sunday broke that open.

The reading at Mass this weekend was the Passion according to Matthew. Matthew 26 and 27. The whole arc — betrayal, denial, abandonment, the cry from the cross. I've heard it before. This year it landed differently.

Eight years ago, when my daughter was eight, I was in California for a customer launch.   My dad called to say he didn't know where she was. I told him to call Missy or the school. He was watching her because Missy was recovering from surgery — and I had chosen to be at work. When she came home hours later, I assumed she was fine. My dad was cold with her, because that's what he knows how to do. I know now what happened in those hours.

Sitting in Mass on Sunday, that memory surfaced while the Passion was being read. Judas choosing money over presence. Peter denying, protecting himself when the cost got too high. The disciples scattering. A child left alone when the people responsible looked away or weren't paying attention or assumed someone else was handling it. My God, my God, why have you forsaken me.

That cry isn't only Christ's. It belongs to every person who has ever been abandoned in a moment that mattered. I heard my daughter in it. I heard myself in it — the man on his phone in California who told his father to figure it out.

It felt like a hole in my chest. Like what I imagine a heart attack feels like. Just darkness, a pressure behind the sternum, a 10/10 feeling like passing out.

Missy was sitting next to me. She felt it too. Afterward we talked about it — how we had both been called in that same Mass to sit with the same memory. How bad things happen in this world. How we worship a God who conquered death. How the pain still exists even though we know the story isn't over.

I want to blame everyone else. And there is blame to go around. But in that church on Sunday, God asked me to sit with my piece of it. I chose work over my family that day eight years ago. That choice had a cost I am only now beginning to feel. Lord, you have shattered my pride. And I find myself turning to you because of it. I am a sinner. Have mercy on me.

There is a story my father used to tell from Hindu mythology. Shiva and Parvati give their two sons a challenge: whoever circles the world three times first wins a prize. Kartik mounts his peacock and flies. Ganesha, slower and rounder, simply walks around his parents three times — because his parents are his world, and circling them is the same as circling the earth. It's a story about wisdom and devotion. The point is that Ganesha understood where the center was.

My father told it as though he was Ganesha. As though the world was meant to circle him.

I pray for him. He is a good man who is hurting so much he cannot see past his own pain. He and my mother took real risks, suffered real losses — the move to a new country, the family they left behind, the grief they never processed. They are good people who became so absorbed in their own pain they couldn't see past it to their children. I hope you find him, God. And when you find him, heal them both.

I have spent years being the older brother to my own parents. Showing up for Sunday dinners after the wedding they skipped. Calling my mother every day. Trying to keep the relationship alive through sheer loyalty. Somewhere underneath that faithfulness — if I'm honest — was the belief that if I just kept showing up, they would eventually choose to be well. That my presence would tip something in them toward repentance, toward healing, toward seeing clearly.

It hasn't worked that way.

The Passion doesn't end at the cross. But it passes through it. The father in Matthew 27 does not intervene to stop the suffering. He lets the consequences land. He trusts the longer story. I am not God and I am not comparing my situation to his. But I am learning something from that posture — that love does not always mean rescue. That presence has limits. That my parents have a choice to make, the same choice the younger son had to make alone in the far country, and I cannot make it for them. I can tell my mother the truth. I can hold a mirror up when I can. I can pray for them and mean it. 

But I cannot pull them back. That is between them and God.

The father in the parable sees his son a long way off and runs. But first his son had to choose to come home. The father didn't go drag him out of the far country. He waited. He watched. He let the consequences land.

This week I am going to talk about this at ManUp. The conflict between being a father and being a son. How I am learning to hold a mirror of truth for my parents — that love is not performance, that if they want a relationship there is a way back and it runs through honesty and through God — while also learning to stand at a distance and watch, without fixing, without controlling, without pretending the watching doesn't cost something.

Lord, I heard you in that Mass. I don't know what you're asking of me yet — about my daughter, about my parents, about the choice I made in California eight years ago. But I'm not running. I'm learning to circle what matters. I'm trying to stay in the room.
Have mercy on me. Amen.
