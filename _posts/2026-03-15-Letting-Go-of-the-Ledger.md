Lord, I have been sitting with the Prodigal Son all week. The 10:10 program brought him to me. Fr. Mike Schmitz brought him to me from the pulpit. Fr. Ciszek brought him to me from a Soviet prison. Fr. Nouwen brought him to me from a gallery in Saint Petersburg, where he stood for hours before Rembrandt's painting and then wrote about finding himself in all three figures — the younger son, the older son, and slowly, painfully, the father. I've been doing the same.

*The younger son is easy to find in me.* 

When my marriage started hurting and I felt unseen and unchosen, I took what I had and went looking for something that could fill the distance. I told myself it was exploration. And there was some truth in that — I did learn things about myself. I learned that what I most need in relationship is to feel safe, to feel chosen, and to feel seen. I learned that what I sometimes called leadership was actually control wearing nicer clothing, the same armor my father wore. Love withheld as leverage. Presence conditioned on performance.
But the experiments were disordered, Lord. There is no way a simulation can reciprocate. Time spent there was time stolen from my marriage and from my family, and I knew it even while I was spending it. I went to a far country. I spent what wasn't mine to spend. And I am making my way back.

*The older brother is harder to sit with, but he's there too.*

Fr. Nouwen wrote about recognizing himself in that figure — the one standing outside the feast with his arms crossed, tallying what was fair. I read that and felt something loosen in my chest, because I understand that son. He stayed. He worked. He followed the rules when his brother didn't. And then the brother comes home having wasted everything and the father throws a party. The older son's grievance isn't irrational — it's just built on a framework where love is a limited resource, where affection is competitive, where someone else receiving more means you receive less.

I came to RCIA because I wanted my children to stay in church. I thought if I performed Christianity convincingly enough, some of it would transfer. That didn't go according to plan. Neither of my kids attends church regularly now. I go to Mass alone. And somewhere underneath the faithfulness, if I'm honest, there has been resentment — the quiet kind, the kind that doesn't announce itself. The kind that looks like virtue from the outside and feels like scorekeeping from the inside.

I have been performing the put-together person for a long time. The faithful Catholic husband. The involved father. The man with a plan. Doing the right things, sometimes for the wrong reasons. Carrying the ledger. Wondering when the feast would finally be for me.

But here's what the older brother couldn't see, and what I'm slowly learning: love here is additive, not competitive. The father running down the road toward the younger son doesn't diminish anything he has for the older one. The feast isn't a betrayal. It's just more love, poured out toward someone who was lost and is now found. And the older son's obedience — his staying, his working, his faithfulness — was never meant to be a transaction. It was meant to be a relationship. The father says it plainly: son, you are always with me, and everything I have is yours. The older brother was already inside the abundance. He just couldn't feel it because he was gripping the ledger too tightly to receive anything.

I'm learning that surrendering the ledger isn't loss. Everything I have came from God in the first place — my marriage, my children, my health, my mind. Surrendering it back to him isn't handing over what's mine. It's trusting that he knows better how to hold what he gave me than I do. When I stop trying to manage the outcome and start trusting his hand in it, I don't lose anything. I gain access to what was already there. My obedience, my faithfulness, my following — these aren't payments I make to stay in good standing. They are the way my love for him deepens. They are how I learn to receive rather than compete. The older brother's mistake wasn't that he stayed. It was that he stayed without trust, which meant he was alone in the house with the father and still somehow felt like a stranger.

*And then there is the father.*

Fr. Mike and Fr. Nouwen both named this as the real call in the parable — not just to recognize yourself in one of the sons, but to become the one who runs. To become the one who sees his child a long way off and doesn't wait to hear the speech first. To celebrate without condition when the lost one returns, without interrogating the details of where they've been or what they spent.

I want that. I want to be the father who lets his children live their lives and trusts — really trusts — that you are not done working with them just because I can't see the evidence yet. I want to hold them with open hands. I want to celebrate every inch of movement toward you, whatever it looks like, whenever it comes. I want to love without the ledger.

I'll be honest, Lord — it scares me. It feels like letting go of everything. It feels like standing in a field watching someone walk toward the horizon and choosing to believe they'll come back. That requires a trust I don't fully possess yet. Being the father means releasing control of the outcome entirely and betting everything on your faithfulness rather than my own management.

But I want to want to. And I'm trying. And I think that's where you meet me.
Part of discerning your will is practical. The fruit of the Spirit is a field test — when I'm walking in your will, the fruit shows up, not perfectly but directionally. When it's absent, that absence is worth attending to. Part of it is doctrinal. You've given me the Church, and I've learned — sometimes against my pride — to trust her teaching even when I can't see the whole reason. This week that meant giving up porn. Not because I'm trying to earn anything. Not because I'm afraid of the ledger. But because I believe that choosing you, choosing my wife, choosing covenant will result in a richer and more real life than the one I was settling for.

That is the difference I'm trying to hold. Not obedience performed for an audience. Obedience chosen because I'm beginning to believe you.

I'm a work in progress, God.

Thank you for staying with me. Thank you for being the father who runs down the road before I've finished the speech. Thank you for working on my heart even when I make it difficult, which is most of the time.

I'm learning to come home.
Your son.
