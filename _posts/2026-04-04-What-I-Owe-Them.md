*Lord, I'm bringing this to you unfinished. I don't have a resolution. I have a wound and a conviction and a theology that I'm trying to hold together. Meet me here.*

---

Paul writes that love is patient, love is kind. That it does not insist on its own way. That it bears all things, believes all things, hopes all things, endures all things.

---

I was sixteen the first time my father threatened to withdraw his support. We were arguing about a girl. He wanted obedience. I wanted to be seen. He told me — plainly, without anger — that if I continued, he would simply stop paying for things. Financial support as leverage. Love as transaction.

I didn't cry. I made a plan. The next morning I started mapping what it would take to survive without him.

I was sixteen and I was already learning that love had conditions.

---

Lord, the Greeks understood something we've flattened. They gave love four names because they knew it wasn't one thing.

*Eros* — the love that ignites. *Storge* — the love that holds family together, the natural bond between parent and child. *Philia* — the love between friends, between equals, built through shared sacrifice and mutual choosing. *Agape* — the love that doesn't need anything back. Willed. Chosen. Ordered toward the good of the other regardless of what you receive.

When Jesus says love your enemies, he's not asking for *storge* that was never given. He's not asking for *philia* that requires two willing people. He's asking for *agape*. A posture. A will. A daily choosing.

I'm trying to understand what that means for me and my parents. I'm not there yet.

---

Senior year of high school, I went on a retreat. Three days, away from everything, with classmates I knew and some I didn't. On the last night something happened that I still can't fully explain.

I felt loved. Unconditionally. Without performing for it.

I came home and told my mother I wanted to convert. I was seventeen and I had just met God and I was full of it.

She took me to India. Three weeks of temples and relatives and the unmistakable message: *you will not become something I don't recognize*. She didn't want a Catholic son. She wanted the son she had planned for.

Lord, I don't say that with bitterness. I say it because it was the first time I understood that the people who love you most can also need you to be someone you're not.

---

The second time my father followed through. I was eighteen. The argument was about obedience again — it was always about obedience. He told me I was defiant. He told me to see if I could make it on my own.

I packed everything I had into a laundry basket. Friends came. We carried it out.

I moved in with a girlfriend. Not because I loved her the way you love a partner. Because she was the only door open.

Lord, I need to be honest about this: that relationship was built on rejection. I wasn't looking for a partner. I was looking for someone who would choose me after my father had un-chosen me. Love built on that foundation isn't love. It's need wearing love's clothes. It was uneven from the start and it broke the way uneven things break — slowly, then all at once, with someone getting hurt.

---

*Agape* doesn't require the other person to change. It doesn't require reconciliation. It doesn't require proximity or trust or safety.

But *philia* does. And *storge* — the familial bond — requires something to grow in. It needs to be tended, both directions. It needs to be safe.

I need to be seen. Chosen. Safe. Those aren't luxuries. They're the conditions under which love can actually take root in me and flow out of me toward anyone else.

We are not called to lose ourselves in love. We are not called to stand in abuse and call it virtue.

Lord, help me hold both truths at once.

---

I went back home after that relationship ended. And here is the thing I have to say clearly, because the story isn't only about the harm:

My father took me back.

For all his words, for all the conditions, he opened the door. He loves me. I believe that. His love has always been real — it's just been distorted by grief he has never been able to name. He lost his parents young. He tried to end his own life before I knew him. He carries shame he can't put down, and when shame rises in him he shuts down, or he reaches for control. He isn't a villain. He's a wounded man who never had anyone show him another way.

The same is true for my mother. She was pushed into choices she didn't make freely. Degrees she didn't want. A marriage she didn't choose. A country that wasn't hers. She carries grief that is tangled up with anger at her own father, whom she still worships, and so the grief never gets processed — it comes out sideways as manipulation, as the need for attention, as creating crises that make her the center.

They are not bad people. They are people who are still bleeding from old wounds and bleeding on everyone near them.

---

Lord, I remember standing at the altar with my wife.

My father didn't come. He told me I wasn't ready. That he couldn't appear and have it seem like he supported something he disagreed with. Love, for him in that moment, was transactional enough that his presence at my wedding had a price — my agreement with his assessment of my readiness.

I got married without him there.

---

After the wedding I did the work of repair. I showed up for weekly dinners. I performed love because I believed love was worth performing until it became real. They aren't strangers to me. They are the people who made me.

But I forced the repair before my wife was ready. I prioritized the reconciliation over her wounds. I told her to forgive before she had the safety to feel the anger. I solved before I sat with her in the pain.

Lord, I'm still paying that cost. In my marriage I am rebuilding something I broke by insisting on fixing something else first.

That's the pattern you keep showing me. *Solving before feeling.* Moving toward what I hope is true before it's been made safe enough to hold.

---

There were years of genuine closeness. My father helped carry us through graduate school — financially, practically, with real care. He wanted us to succeed. That wasn't performance. That was love doing what it could with the tools he had.

My mother asked me, when I told her I was completing RCIA and bringing my family fully into the Church — she asked me to promise I would help her have a Hindu burial someday. I said yes. I meant it.

---

When our trials came in 2024 I told them. I asked for support. They offered everything they had.

Everything except what I needed most: to be emotionally held. To be seen completely. To be chosen without conditions.

They couldn't. Not because they didn't want to. Because they are still in the middle of their own wounding, and wounded people can only give from what they have healed.

---

The last rupture came over a trip. The details don't matter as much as this: my father needed me to show up in a particular way. I couldn't. I was barely holding together. I asked him to see me in that — to stand with me while my life was coming apart, to be the father I needed, not the one he was comfortable being.

He shut down. He cut me off again.

And I found myself alone with my family sick in a hotel room, family members waiting for us to perform normalcy — and I had to choose. Protect my family. Hold the boundary. Let go of being the son they needed in that moment.

Lord, I have never felt more alone.

---

It hurts. I want to say that without qualification. It hurts not to talk to them. It hurts to love people I cannot safely be near right now.

And I know this about myself: I am not immune to their patterns. When they are in my life I want to please them. That wanting is strong enough to make me someone my wife and children cannot fully count on. I become the boy trying to earn his place again, and the man I've fought to become recedes.

I am protecting my children from something I haven't fully escaped myself.

---

*Agape*, Lord. Not because I've arrived at it. Because you're calling me toward it.

I love my parents. I believe their love for me is real. I believe they are carrying wounds that were given to them before I was born. I believe that one day — your timing, not mine — there may be a way back to something.

But I cannot be the son they need right now. Not if I'm going to be the father and husband I'm called to be.

---

*Lord, meet me here. I'm tired of trying to numb the pain alone. I love them. I can't be near them. I don't know how to hold both of those things except in your hands.*

Amen.
